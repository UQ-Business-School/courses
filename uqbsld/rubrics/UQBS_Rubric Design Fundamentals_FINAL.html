Rubric Design Fundamentals · A six-part guide

Build a better rubric.

A rubric is only as useful as its ability to do two things: help students understand what they're aiming for before they submit, and help markers reach consistent grades independently. This guide shows you how.

Whether writing from scratch or reviewing a rubric you've used for years — these principles apply to any assessment type.

01 · The Core Problem

Why most rubrics fail before they start

The single most common issue in rubric design is adverb scaling — differentiating grades by swapping adjectives (excellent → very good → good → satisfactory) rather than describing what the student actually does differently at each level.

"Adverb scaling is so prevalent because it's easy to write. But this convenience produces rubrics that are subjective for markers and opaque for students."

What adverb scaling looks like

A criterion written with adverb scaling ↓
HD (7)D (6)C (5)P (4)
Critical Analysis Demonstrates excellent critical analysis with outstanding depth Demonstrates very good critical analysis with considerable depth Demonstrates good critical analysis with adequate depth Demonstrates satisfactory critical analysis with basic depth

The only things changing are the adverbs. A student has no concrete guidance on what to do to improve. A marker has no observable criteria to anchor their judgement.

What observable descriptors look like

The same criterion rewritten with observable descriptors ↓
HD (7)D (6)C (5)P (4)
Critical Analysis Integrates theory to explain why issues occur; connects factors to show how they reinforce each other; identifies limitations in the theory applied Applies theory to explain issues; identifies relationships between factors; analysis is coherent and well-structured Applies relevant theory to the case; addresses multiple factors but treats them somewhat independently Links case to theory but connections are surface-level; factors listed rather than analysed

Each grade now describes a qualitatively different action. A student can read this and know exactly what to do to improve — and two markers can independently reach the same grade.

The reliability test

Ask two questions of any rubric. First: could a student use this to improve their work before submission? Second: would two markers independently reach the same grade? If your descriptors rely on adverbs, both answers are no.

02 · Values of Inquiry

A language for evaluating thinking quality

The Values of Inquiry (VoI) framework, developed by Dr Peter Ellerton at the UQ Critical Thinking Project, provides a language for evaluating the quality of thinking. It identifies eight values — things we value in the act of inquiry — that generate precise, actionable rubric descriptors.

Ellerton deliberately uses values rather than standards because values are general, overlapping, and contextually applied. Like a graphic equaliser, different types of inquiry foreground different values — a quantitative analysis foregrounds precision and accuracy; a literature review foregrounds depth and coherence.

Clarity
Making points as clear as possible; ensuring the audience can understand what we mean
Accuracy
Representing all information correctly and closely aligned with its original meaning
Precision
Being specific and intentional with language to remove potential for misunderstanding
Depth
Providing detailed information to thoroughly develop points; addressing complexities
Breadth
Covering a diverse range of directly relevant content and considerations
Relevance
Choosing information that relates directly to the points being developed
Significance
Discussing the most important information; focusing on key ideas, not superficial detail
Coherence
Structuring arguments logically so each point builds towards intended meaning

VoI replacing adverbs — worked example

Evidence use in a research essay — differentiated by VoI values
GradeDescriptor
HDSources selected for their significance to the argument; evidence integrated with precision; counter-evidence addressed with breadth and depth
DSources relevant and accurately represented (accuracy); evidence supports claims with clarity; some counter-evidence acknowledged
CSources relevant but used descriptively rather than analytically; evidence–claim connections lack precision
PSources present but selection lacks relevance or significance; evidence listed rather than integrated

Each grade is differentiated by which VoI values are present, absent, or developing — not by adverbs. The vocabulary does the work that "excellent/good/satisfactory" was trying to do, but with specificity both students and markers can act on.

"The goal is to internalise these values so your descriptors naturally describe observable, qualitatively different actions at each grade level."

03 · SOLO Taxonomy

Mapping structural complexity across the grade scale

SOLO (Structure of Observed Learning Outcomes, Biggs & Collis 1982) describes five levels of increasing structural complexity. Where VoI provides a language for the quality of thinking, SOLO describes the structure — how students organise and connect ideas. Together they are complementary lenses, not competing frameworks.

SOLO LevelUQ GradeWhat the student does
Extended abstractD–HD (6–7)Goes beyond the given; generalises, hypothesises, or critiques; connects to broader principles; identifies limitations
RelationalC–D (5–6)Integrates multiple aspects into a coherent whole; relationships, causes, and effects are identified
Multi-structuralP–C (4–5)Identifies several relevant aspects but treats them independently; lists or catalogues without connecting
Uni-structuralP (4)Focuses on one relevant aspect; addresses the task from a single perspective
Pre-structuralF (1–3)Misses the point or uses irrelevant information; the response does not address the task

SOLO in rubric descriptors

Problem Identification — SOLO progression for a case study report
GradeDescriptor
HDExtended abstract: Identifies issues not explicitly stated in the case; connects them to broader organisational and contextual factors; distinguishes symptoms from root causes and prioritises by impact
DRelational: Identifies key issues accurately and connects them to the broader organisational context; explains how issues relate to one another
CMulti-structural: Identifies key issues within the relevant context; addresses multiple issues but treats them somewhat independently
PUni-structural: Identifies issues stated in the case; addresses the task from a single perspective without contextualisation
F–3Pre-structural: Attempts to identify issues but misinterprets the case or focuses on peripheral details; the core problem is not addressed
F–1/2Pre-structural: Does not identify relevant issues; the response does not engage with the case or is entirely off-topic

The SOLO progression is visible: at Pass the student works with what's explicitly given; at Credit they catalogue multiple issues; at Distinction they connect them; at HD they go beyond the given material to identify implicit issues and prioritise. This is a qualitative shift in cognitive operation, not just "more."

"SOLO tells you not just how much a student knows, but what they can do with what they know."

Important — differentiated fail descriptors: Under UQ's Assessment Procedures, students who receive a grade 3 (45–49%) are eligible for supplementary assessment. Students who receive a 1 or 2 are not. A single "Fail" column cannot make this distinction — rubrics must describe these separately.

04 · Writing Descriptors

The spine-and-distinguisher structure

Strong descriptor sets share a spine — a common sentence structure that stays consistent across grade levels — with distinguishers that change to indicate the qualitative shift. The spine anchors the reader. The distinguishers show what changes across grades.

Spine (highlighted) + distinguishers
HDIdentifies issues at multiple levels; distinguishes symptoms from root causes; prioritises by impact
DIdentifies issues accurately and connects them to the broader organisational context
CIdentifies issues stated in the case within the relevant context
PIdentifies issues stated in the case, though without contextualisation

A process for writing descriptors

1
Name the capability. What cognitive skill is this criterion asking the student to demonstrate? Analysis? Application? Evaluation? Communication?
2
Describe "excellent" in observable terms. What would you actually see in the student's work? Use VoI to guide you — which values are fully present at HD?
3
Describe "minimally acceptable." What is the Pass threshold? Which VoI values are present but limited? What SOLO level is the student operating at?
4
Fill in the middle grades. Use SOLO progression: listing → connecting → integrating → critiquing.
5
Check for adverb creep. If the only difference between two adjacent grades is an adjective or adverb, rewrite to describe an observable difference in what the student produces.

05 · Structural Design

Criteria that describe capability, not sections

A common issue is organising rubric rows by sections of the assessment — Introduction, Literature Review, Analysis, Conclusion — rather than by capabilities the student is demonstrating. Section-based criteria create several problems: the same capability gets assessed in multiple rows, students are penalised for organising their work thoughtfully, and descriptors are difficult to differentiate across grade levels.

Section-based (before)
Row 1: Introduction (10%)
Row 2: Literature Review (20%)
Row 3: Analysis (30%)
Row 4: Recommendations (20%)
Row 5: Conclusion (10%)
Row 6: Referencing (10%)
6 rows mirroring document structure. Critical analysis assessed in Rows 2, 3, and arguably 4. Introduction and Conclusion rows hard to differentiate across grades.
Capability-based (after)
Row 1: Problem Identification (20%)
Row 2: Critical Analysis (35%)
Row 3: Application to Practice (25%)
Row 4: Academic Communication (20%)
4 rows assessing distinct capabilities. Each row has a clear, differentiable progression. Weightings reflect importance to learning outcomes.

"If your rubric can be gamed by a student who has neither read deeply nor thought carefully, rewrite it."

06 · Accessibility

Writing for diverse student populations

UQ's student body includes a significant proportion of students from culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) backgrounds. Rubric language that is idiomatic, culturally specific, or unnecessarily complex creates barriers unrelated to capability.

Practical guidelines
Use plain, direct language. Replace "cogently proposes innovative solutions" with "solutions follow logically from the analysis; each recommendation is justified with evidence."
Avoid idiomatic expressions. Phrases like "hits the mark" or "leaves much to be desired" are culturally loaded and imprecise. Describe what the student produces, not how it makes you feel.
Be specific about quantity where it matters. "Extensive use of the literature" is subjective; "Draws on at least 12 peer-reviewed sources" is concrete. Use count anchors at the Pass threshold.
Ensure each cell can stand alone. A student should be able to read a single cell and understand what's expected without cross-referencing other cells. Avoid "better than Credit-level work."

"Rubric language that is idiomatic or culturally specific creates barriers that have nothing to do with the student's capability in the assessed skill."

07 · Final check

What your grade profile tells you

Rubric design and grade distributions are connected. If an assessment consistently produces grades clustered at one end of the scale, the rubric and assessment task should be reviewed — the issue is almost certainly with design, not students.

A well-calibrated assessment centres its cohort at Pass–Credit. The rubric's Pass and Credit descriptors carry the most weight.

Reading your distribution

Diagnostic guide
Too many high grades (>35% at 6–7): Check whether your descriptors genuinely differentiate Credit, Distinction, and HD. If the HD descriptor is essentially "Distinction but more so," the rubric may be collapsing those grades together.
Too many failures (>20%): Review the Pass threshold. Could a student read the Pass descriptor and know what "good enough" looks like? Check whether the assessment aligns with what was actually taught.
Grades clustered at one level: Use VoI values and SOLO levels to create observable qualitative shifts between adjacent grades.
No students reaching HD: HD should represent the top of what is achievable within the task — not a theoretical ideal no student could reach.

"The rubric is the theory. The distribution is the data. When they conflict, trust the data."

Quick self-check — 9 audit questions

1
Could a student use this rubric to improve their work before submission?
2
Would two markers independently reach the same grade?
3
Does each grade level describe a qualitatively different action?
4
Are your rubric rows organised by capability rather than by section?
5
Can you read across a row and see a clear SOLO progression?
6
Could you identify which VoI values each descriptor addresses?
7
Would a CALD student understand every descriptor without cultural assumptions?
8
Does your rubric include differentiated fail descriptors distinguishing grade 3 from grades 1–2?
9
Is your grade distribution within UQ expectations?

If you answer "no" to three or more, the rubric would benefit from revision. Contact the UQ Business School Learning Design team to arrange a Rubric Surgery workshop.
