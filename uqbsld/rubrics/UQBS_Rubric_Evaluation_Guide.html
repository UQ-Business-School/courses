Evaluating Rubric Outputs

A structured guide for reviewing rubrics — whether they've been generated by AI, drafted by a colleague, or revised from an existing version.

Work through four focus areas: GA criteria accuracy, constructive alignment, language and clarity, and practical usability. Each section explains what to look for and why it matters.

Section 1

Graduate Attribute Criteria Accuracy

Getting the GA criteria right is essential — they feed directly into AACSB Assurance of Learning reporting. Start by checking the basics.

1.1 Correct GA Selection and Criteria Count

Before checking anything else, determine what type of course you're dealing with. This affects how many sub-criteria are required.

Is this an AoL-designated course or a general GA mapping course? AoL courses use the rubric as a formal data-collection instrument for AACSB reporting. General GA mapping courses integrate GA criteria for curriculum uplift but aren't used for formal AoL data collection. This distinction matters for the sub-criteria count.
Are the correct GAs included? Cross-check against the course's official GA mapping. The GAs in the rubric must match what is mapped for this specific assessment task.
How many GAs are represented? The standard is two GAs per course. However, there are legitimate exceptions — some courses map only one GA to a rubric-assessed task. Verify the number against the official mapping rather than assuming two.
Is the sub-criteria count correct for this course type? For AoL-designated courses, all three sub-criteria from each GA are required. For general GA mapping courses, two of the three is standard.

1.2 Faithful Representation and Contextualisation

The GA criteria should preserve the intent of the original templates while being translated into assessment-specific language.

Do the GA criteria retain the core intent of the template descriptors? The wording doesn't need to be identical, but the intellectual substance must be preserved.
Has the template language been contextualised to this specific assessment? The GA templates use intentionally generic language that cannot be used verbatim. If the descriptors still read like the generic template, the contextualisation hasn't happened. Check for both: fidelity to GA intent and specificity to the task.
Has the "smush" distorted the GA criteria? When GA criteria are blended with existing rubric criteria, there's a risk the GA elements get diluted. Check that GA-specific requirements are still clearly identifiable.
Are all seven grade levels written for each GA criterion? Every GA criterion needs descriptors at every level (HD through LF) — no gaps. Descriptors should progress logically, not just repeat the same idea with weaker adverbs.

1.3 Weighting and Marking Requirements

Do the GA criteria collectively account for at least 10% of the assessment's marks?
Are the asterisk markers (*) present on all GA criteria names?
Is the GA attribution footer present? The rubric should include a footer note explaining what the asterisk means and which GAs are being assessed.
Section 2

Constructive Alignment

A beautifully written rubric is useless if it's measuring the wrong things. This section checks whether the rubric criteria actually match the assessment task and learning outcomes.

2.1 Alignment with Assessment Task

Does every criterion relate to something the assessment task actually requires?
Are all major components of the task represented in the rubric?
Are the criteria capability-based rather than section-based? Criteria should describe intellectual capabilities (e.g., "Critical Analysis," "Evidence Integration") rather than assignment sections (e.g., "Introduction," "Body," "Conclusion").

2.2 Alignment with Learning Outcomes

Can you trace each criterion back to a course learning outcome?
Are the CLOs that this assessment is mapped to adequately covered?
Is the cognitive level consistent with the CLOs? If a CLO says students will "evaluate" or "create," the rubric's HD descriptors should require evaluation or creation — not just description or application.

2.3 Course Level Calibration

The same criterion at the same grade level should look different depending on whether you're assessing a first-year undergraduate or a postgraduate student. An HD descriptor for a first-year course is not the same standard as an HD for a 7000-level course.

Are the descriptor expectations appropriate for the course level?
Is the baseline (Pass level) realistic for the cohort?

2.4 Bloom's Taxonomy Progression

Do the grade levels require progressively higher-order thinking? The progression should move up through Bloom's levels: remembering and understanding at lower grades, analysing and evaluating at higher grades, and creating or synthesising at HD.
Does the rubric avoid rewarding quantity instead of quality? "More examples" or "more sources" is not higher-order thinking. Higher grades should require different kinds of thinking, not just more of the same kind.

2.5 Assessment Type-Specific Checks

Different assessment types have different pitfalls. Check the common issues for your type:

Essays and reports: Are criteria capability-based? Does the rubric avoid rewarding length over depth?
Presentations: Is content weighted appropriately relative to delivery? Are delivery criteria culturally inclusive?
Reflective tasks: Does the rubric assess the quality of analytical reflection, not the intensity of personal disclosure?
Vivas and practicals: Consider whether the Dreyfus Model (novice → expert) is more appropriate than Bloom's for assessing professional skill development.

2.6 Requirements Embedding

Are task requirements (word count, source minimums, formatting) embedded within descriptors? Meeting the source minimum should sit at Credit level; exceeding it with seminal works at HD. Requirements should not be listed separately from the rubric.
Section 3

Language and Clarity

Rubrics fail when markers interpret descriptors differently or when students can't understand what's expected. This section checks whether the language is clear, specific, and accessible.

3.1 The Adverb Trap

This is the single most common problem in rubrics. Check carefully.

Do the descriptors differentiate levels using observable behaviours rather than adverbs? If the main difference between levels is words like "thoroughly," "adequately," "somewhat," or "clearly," the rubric has fallen into the adverb trap.
Could two markers read the same descriptor and agree on what it means?
What would a student learn from reading this rubric? A well-written rubric teaches students what higher performance looks like. "Demonstrates adequate understanding" teaches nothing. A good descriptor tells them what to do differently.

3.2 Specificity and Measurability

Do the descriptors describe what is present in the work, not just how good it is? "Excellent analysis" is a judgement. "Identifies multiple causal factors and explains how they interact" is a description.
Do the descriptors use action verbs? Look for "identifies," "evaluates," "integrates," "proposes," "critiques." Be wary of adjectives doing the heavy lifting.
Are count metrics used appropriately? "Identifies 4 or more factors" is a quantity proxy, not a quality measure. Counts are fine for task requirements (source minimums at Credit) but not as stand-ins for analytical depth.

3.3 Logical Progression Across Levels

Is there a clear qualitative difference between adjacent grade levels?
Can you identify the spine and distinguishers? Descriptors should share a consistent stem (the "spine") with qualitatively different elements (the "distinguishers") that change at each level. If the spine shifts unpredictably, that's a readability problem.
Which differentiation lever is each criterion scaling? The three levers are Scope (how wide?), Complexity (how connected?), and Autonomy (how independent?). Check that the choice is consistent across grade levels within each criterion.
Are negative definitions used to clarify boundaries? E.g., "Unlike the Distinction level, the Credit level relies primarily on assigned course readings and does not incorporate independent research." Look for this especially at the C/D and D/HD boundaries.
Are the Fail and Marginal Fail descriptors substantive? These levels should describe what the work looks like, not just say "does not meet requirements."

3.4 CALD Accessibility

Is jargon minimised or explained?
Are idioms and culturally specific expressions avoided?
Are delivery criteria (for presentations) culturally inclusive? Avoid requiring sustained eye contact, assertive body language, or extroverted delivery styles.
Is grammatical accuracy assessed separately from content quality? Separating expression from content improves marking reliability for all students — not just CALD inclusion. If language quality matters, give it its own criterion.
Section 4

Practical Usability

Will the rubric actually work — for markers using it to assess, for students using it to prepare, and for coordinators using it to maintain consistency?

4.1 Structure and Scope

Is the number of criteria appropriate? Aim for 3–6. Fewer than 3 is too broad; more than 6 is too granular.
Do the weightings reflect relative importance?
Do the weightings add up to exactly 100%?

4.2 Coordinator Buy-In

Would the coordinator recognise their rubric in this output? If the original has been substantially reworked, check whether recognisable elements have been preserved. A rubric that feels entirely foreign will face resistance.
Has the revision preserved what was already working? The approach should be "remix rather than scrap."
Are the changes defensible? Every significant departure from the original should be traceable to a design principle.

4.3 Marking Feasibility

Could a sessional marker use this rubric with minimal training?
Are the descriptors the right length? Generally 1–3 sentences per descriptor. If a single descriptor is a full paragraph, it's probably doing too much.
Is there enough differentiation between adjacent levels?

4.4 Student Readability

Could a student understand what HD-level work looks like?
Does the rubric help students self-assess?
Is the rubric self-contained? Students shouldn't need a separate document to understand expectations.

4.5 Formatting and Presentation

Document header contains: course code, course name, semester, assessment number, name, and weighting?
UQ formatting applied correctly? Purple header row, alternating row colours, grade headers with percentage ranges, criterion names with weightings.
File named correctly? Standard convention: [CourseCode]_A[Number]_Rubric_[GA].docx
Getting Started

How to Use This Guide

For Learning Designers

Work through all four sections before sending a rubric to an academic for review. Sections 1 and 2 are your primary responsibility — academics will rely on your expertise here.

For Academics and Course Coordinators

Focus on sections 2 and 4 first. You're best placed to judge whether the rubric aligns with what you actually want students to do (section 2) and whether it will work in your marking context (section 4).

For Collaborative Review

Start with section 2 (constructive alignment) as common ground, then divide the remaining sections based on expertise.

When you find issues: Note the specific criterion, level, and what needs to change. Be as concrete as possible — "the D descriptor for Critical Analysis needs to differentiate from C by requiring integration of perspectives, not just listing them" is far more actionable than "the middle levels need work."
