Chapter 01

The Crime Scene

The single most common problem in rubric design has a name. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

It's called adverb scaling, and it works like this: you write a beautiful descriptor for your top grade. Then, to create the lower grades, you just… soften the adjectives.

Excellent becomes very good becomes good becomes satisfactory.

It's quick. It's easy. And it's almost completely useless.

Exhibit A: The Adverb Rubric

Here's a real pattern from a case study report. See if you can spot what changes between grades.

⚠ Before — Adverb Scaling
HD (7) D (6) C (5) P (4)
Critical Analysis Demonstrates excellent critical analysis of the case with outstanding depth Demonstrates very good critical analysis of the case with considerable depth Demonstrates good critical analysis of the case with adequate depth Demonstrates satisfactory critical analysis of the case with basic depth

The only things changing are the highlighted adverbs. A student reading this has no concrete guidance on what to do to move from a Pass to a Distinction. A marker has no observable criteria to distinguish between grades.

The Fix: Observable Descriptors

Now look at the same criterion rewritten so that each grade describes a qualitatively different action.

✓ After — Observable Descriptors
HD (7) D (6) C (5) P (4)
Critical Analysis Integrates theory to explain why issues occur; connects factors to show how they reinforce each other; analysis extends beyond the case Applies theory to explain issues; identifies relationships between factors; analysis addresses complexity Applies relevant theory to the case; addresses multiple factors but treats them independently Links case to theory but connections are surface-level; factors listed rather than analysed

Each grade now describes a qualitatively different action. At Pass, the student lists factors. At Credit, they address multiple factors but independently. At Distinction, they identify relationships. At HD, they integrate and extend beyond the case.

The reliability test: Could a student use this rubric to improve their work before submission? Would two markers independently reach the same grade? If both answers are "yes," the rubric is working.

So how do you systematically write descriptors like this? You need two frameworks. Let's meet them.

Chapter 02

The Values of Inquiry

A language for evaluating the quality of thinking — not just whether thinking happened.

The Values of Inquiry (VoI) framework, developed by Dr Peter Ellerton at the UQ Critical Thinking Project, identifies eight values that describe what makes thinking good. A student may be performing the cognitive skill of analysis — but that doesn't mean they're doing it well.

VoI gives you the vocabulary to describe the difference. Instead of scaling adverbs, you describe which values are present and to what degree.

The Eight Values

Click any card to explore the guiding questions.

🔍 Clarity
Making points as clear as possible for the audience.

Is the argument structure clear? Are words well-defined and unambiguous? Are examples used effectively?

✔ Accuracy
Representing all information correctly and faithfully.

Are claims justified? Is what you are saying true? Have you represented ideas faithfully?

🎯 Precision
Being specific and intentional with language and terminology.

Have you used technical terms appropriately? Have you quantified where appropriate? Could you be more specific?

⛏ Depth
Providing detailed information to thoroughly develop points.

Are complexities sufficiently described? Have problematic aspects been identified? Is there engagement with underlying causes?

🌐 Breadth
Covering a diverse range of directly relevant content.

Have you considered alternative perspectives? Why have you preferred one perspective over another?

➡ Relevance
Choosing information that relates directly to the point being made.

Have you focused on the point at issue? Is distracting or unhelpful information included?

⭐ Significance
Discussing the most important information related to the topic.

Have you avoided superficial issues? Have you identified and developed your core points?

🔗 Coherence
Structuring arguments so each point builds logically on the last.

Are ideas developed logically? Do all premises support your conclusions? Are logical fallacies avoided?

Why This Changes Everything

VoI translates directly to rubric descriptors. Instead of saying a student's analysis is "excellent" or "satisfactory," you describe which values are present and to what degree.

The values overlap and interact. Precision contributes to clarity. Depth benefits from coherence. Breadth without relevance produces unfocused work. This means your rubric criteria should foreground different combinations of values depending on what the assessment demands.

For a quantitative analysis task, you might foreground precision and accuracy. For a stakeholder analysis, breadth and significance. For a literature review, depth, coherence, and relevance. VoI doesn't prescribe — it gives you a vocabulary to make deliberate choices about what quality looks like in your context.

Chapter 03

SOLO Taxonomy

If VoI describes the quality of thinking, SOLO describes its structural complexity — and maps directly to the UQ grading scale.

The Structure of the Observed Learning Outcome (SOLO) Taxonomy, developed by Biggs and Collis (1982), describes five levels of increasing structural complexity. SOLO is useful for rubric design because it describes a natural progression in how students handle complexity.

Click any level to see a worked example for a case study.

Extended Abstract
Goes beyond the given
Distinction – HD • 75–100%
The student generalises, hypothesises, or critically evaluates — transferring understanding to new contexts. Connections are made to broader principles.

Problem Identification: "Identifies issues at multiple levels; draws on theory from outside the case to explain why these issues interact; proposes original framing."

Relational
Integrates into a coherent whole
Credit – Distinction • 65–84%
The student integrates aspects into a coherent whole. Relationships, causes, and effects are explained. Understanding is structured.

Problem Identification: "Identifies relationships between issues; explains how operational problems create downstream financial risk."

Multi-Structural
Several relevant aspects, independently
Pass – Credit • 50–74%
The student identifies several relevant aspects but treats them independently. The response is a list rather than an integrated argument.

Problem Identification: "Identifies several issues from the case — leadership, financial, operational — but discusses each in isolation."

Uni-Structural
One relevant aspect
Pass • 50–64%
The student focuses on one relevant aspect. The response addresses the task but is limited and surface-level.

Problem Identification: "Identifies the key issue stated in the case but does not explore contributing factors."

Pre-Structural
Misses the point
Fail • 0–49%
The student misses the point or uses irrelevant information. The response does not address the task in a meaningful way.

Problem Identification: "Restates case facts without identifying any issues, or identifies issues unrelated to the case."

VoI + SOLO: The Complete Toolkit

These two frameworks are complementary, not competing. In practice:

SOLO establishes the grade-level architecture — it tells you what kind of cognitive operation to describe at each level (listing, connecting, critiquing).

VoI provides the descriptor language — it tells you what qualities to look for within that operation (depth, breadth, precision, coherence).

A descriptor using both frameworks might read: "Integrates theory to explain why issues occur [relational/extended abstract]; connects factors to show how they reinforce each other [coherence]; identifies implications that go beyond the immediate case [significance]."

Chapter 04

Writing Observable Descriptors

The spine-and-distinguisher structure — a practical method for translating VoI and SOLO into rubric cells.

Strong descriptor sets share a spine — a common sentence structure or opening that stays consistent across grade levels — with distinguishers that change to indicate the qualitative shift.

The Spine in Action

HD Identifies issues at multiple levels; draws on theory from outside the case to explain why these issues interact; proposes original framing.
D Identifies issues and explains relationships between them; analysis connects operational, financial, and strategic dimensions.
C Identifies issues across several categories (e.g., leadership, financial, operational) but discusses each in isolation.
P Identifies issues that are explicitly stated in the case but does not explore contributing factors or underlying causes.

The grey text is the spine. The green text is the distinguisher — what changes at each grade level.

The 6-Step Process

01

Name the capability

What cognitive skill is this criterion asking the student to demonstrate? Analysis? Application? Evaluation? Communication?

02

Describe "excellent" in observable terms

Write the HD descriptor first. What would you actually see in the student's work? Use VoI to identify which values are fully present.

03

Describe "minimally acceptable"

Write the Pass descriptor. What is the threshold? Which VoI values are present but limited? What SOLO level is the student operating at?

04

Fill in the middle grades

Use SOLO progression to structure the journey from Pass to HD. Map the qualitative shifts: listing → connecting → integrating → critiquing.

05

Check the spine

Read across the row. Is there a consistent structure that makes the progression visible? If you underline the common elements, do the distinguishers tell a coherent story?

06

Check for adverb creep

Review each descriptor. If the only difference between two adjacent grades is an adjective or adverb, rewrite to describe an observable difference in the work.

Chapter 05

Capabilities, Not Sections

Your rubric rows shouldn't mirror your assignment template. They should assess what students can do.

A common structural problem: organising rubric rows by sections of the assessment (Introduction, Literature Review, Analysis, Conclusion) rather than by the capabilities being assessed.

This creates real issues. The same capability often gets assessed in multiple rows — critical analysis might appear in "Literature Review," "Analysis," and "Discussion." Students are anchored to rigid structures rather than rewarded for the quality of their thinking.

✗ Section-Based

  • ● Row 1: Introduction (10%)
  • ● Row 2: Literature Review (20%)
  • ● Row 3: Analysis (30%)
  • ● Row 4: Discussion (20%)
  • ● Row 5: Conclusion (10%)
  • ● Row 6: Formatting (10%)

6 rows mirroring document structure. Critical analysis assessed in rows 2, 3, and 4 with different language each time.

✓ Capability-Based

  • ● Row 1: Problem Identification (20%)
  • ● Row 2: Critical Analysis (35%)
  • ● Row 3: Application to Practice (25%)
  • ● Row 4: Communication (20%)

4 rows assessing distinct capabilities. Each row has a clear, differentiable progression.

The capability-based version is more efficient and each row is genuinely assessable with observable descriptors. It's difficult to write meaningfully different HD and Distinction descriptors for "Introduction" — but straightforward for "Problem Identification."

Capability-based criteria also map naturally to program-level learning outcomes and graduate attributes, supporting institutional reporting and AACSB Assurance of Learning requirements.

Chapter 06

Writing for Everyone

UQ's student body is culturally and linguistically diverse. Your rubric language should be too — which means making it simpler, not more complicated.

Rubric language that is idiomatic, culturally specific, or unnecessarily complex creates a comprehension barrier that disadvantages students from culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) backgrounds — regardless of their actual capability.

💬

Use plain, direct language

Avoid inflated academic prose in your descriptors. The rubric is a communication tool, not a showcase.

"Cogently proposes innovative solutions" "Solutions follow logically from the analysis; each recommendation is justified with evidence."
🚫

Avoid idiomatic expressions

Phrases like "hits the mark," "scrapes by," or "leaves much to be desired" are culturally loaded and imprecise. Describe what the student produces, not how it makes you feel.

📖

Define technical terms

If your rubric uses discipline-specific terminology (e.g., "stakeholder analysis," "sensitivity testing"), ensure the assessment description or a supporting resource defines these terms.

🔢

Be specific about quantity

If you expect a minimum number of sources, state it.

"Extensive use of the literature" "Draws on at least 12 peer-reviewed sources published within the last 10 years."
📋

Each cell must stand alone

A student should be able to read a single cell (e.g., Distinction for Critical Analysis) and understand what's expected without needing to cross-reference other cells or grade levels.

Diagnostic Guide

Each pattern in your grade distribution points to a specific rubric issue.

⬆

Too many high grades (>35% at 6–7)

Check whether your descriptors genuinely differentiate between Credit, Distinction, and HD. If the HD descriptor is essentially "Distinction but more so," the rubric isn't doing its job. Use SOLO extended abstract features to separate HD from D.

⬇

Too many failures (>20%)

Review the Pass threshold. Is it clearly described? Could a student read the Pass descriptor and know what "good enough" looks like? Also check whether the assessment task allows students to demonstrate the required capabilities.

↔

Grades clustered at one level

The rubric may lack sufficient differentiation. Use VoI values and SOLO levels to create observable qualitative shifts between adjacent grades.

🚫

No students reaching HD

Are your HD descriptors describing genuinely outstanding work, or an impossible standard? HD should represent the top of what is achievable within the task constraints, not perfection.

Chapter 08

The Rubric Health Check

Nine questions. Be honest. Click each one to mark it as a "yes" or a "no" for your current rubric.

Click once for yes. Click again for no. Click a third time to reset. If you answer "no" to three or more, your rubric would benefit from revision.

Could a student use this rubric to improve their work before submission?
If your descriptors say "excellent" and "satisfactory," the answer is probably no.
Would two markers independently reach the same grade?
If descriptors rely on subjective judgments like "very good" versus "good," marker agreement will be low.
Does each grade level describe a qualitatively different action?
Moving from "good" to "very good" is an adverb shift. Moving from "lists factors" to "connects factors" is a qualitative shift.
Are your rubric rows organised by capability rather than by section?
If rows mirror the document structure (Introduction, Literature Review, Analysis…), critical analysis may be fragmented across multiple rows.
Can you read across a row and see a clear SOLO progression?
Use the spine-and-distinguisher structure. The spine stays constant; the distinguishers show qualitative shifts.
Could you identify which VoI values each descriptor addresses?
If you can't name the values at play (clarity, depth, coherence…), the descriptor may be too vague to be useful.
Would a CALD student understand every descriptor without cultural assumptions?
Check for idioms, culturally specific references, and unnecessarily complex language.
Does your rubric include differentiated fail descriptors?
A single "Fail" column isn't sufficient. UQ's 7-point scale includes three fail grades (1, 2, 3) with distinct implications for supplementary assessment.
Is your grade distribution within UQ expectations?
No more than 35% at 6–7; majority at 4–5; no more than 20% failing. If not, the rubric is the first place to investigate.
