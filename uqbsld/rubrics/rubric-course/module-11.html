Calibration & Context

Floors and Ceilings: The Realism Check

A rubric can be brilliant in theory but impossible in practice. The Pass floor is too high if typical second-year students can't reach it. The HD ceiling is too high if students can't achieve it within the task constraints (word limit, time, scope).

Pass Floor: Can a typical, engaged second-year student achieve a Pass? If the descriptor asks for independent reading, literature synthesis, or advanced skills beyond their course level, the floor is too high. A Pass should be realistic for any student who has attended and engaged.

HD Ceiling: Can a student achieve HD within the constraints you've set? If you set a 2,000-word limit but your HD descriptor implies needing 5,000 words of synthesis, the ceiling is unreachable. If you require 30 sources but give 8 weeks, it's impossible.

Activity 3: Adjacent Level Test

Two adjacent levels (Credit and Distinction) often blur together. Show what makes them distinct by writing a negative definition: what Distinction requires that Credit does not.

3
Sharpen the Boundary Between Levels

Credit Level

Identifies multiple factors relevant to the problem. Connects each factor to a course theory. Explains how factors contribute to the observed outcome.

Distinction Level

Identifies multiple causes and explains how they interact to produce outcomes. Demonstrates how different theoretical perspectives illuminate different aspects of the problem.

Your task: Write one clarifying sentence using a negative definition. Start: "Unlike the Distinction level, the Credit level does not require..."
