Capability-Based Criteria

Academics naturally structure rubrics like assignments — Introduction, Body, Conclusion. This module breaks that habit.

Activity 2 of 4

Same Work, Two Rubrics

Here's a short excerpt of student work. Grade it against the section-based rubric, then against a capability-based rubric. Notice which gives you more useful information.

2
Compare Two Approaches

"TechFlow's turnover is driven by a combination of poor management communication (Herzberg) and a lack of developmental opportunities (Maslow). While management's salary increase addresses Herzberg's hygiene factors, it ignores motivational factors entirely. I recommend implementing a mentoring programme, creating visible promotion pathways, and conducting regular staff feedback surveys. However, these recommendations assume resources that a mid-size company may not have, so a phased approach starting with the lowest-cost intervention would be prudent."

Section-Based Rubric

Introduction: C — Good introduction

Analysis: D — Very good analysis

Recommendations: C — Good recommendations

Referencing: P — Satisfactory

What does this tell the student? Almost nothing.

Capability-Based Rubric

Problem Identification: D — Identifies both immediate and systemic factors

Critical Analysis: C — Applies theories but doesn't explain their interaction

Evidence Integration: P — Cites theories but doesn't synthesise across sources

Recommendations: D — Feasibility-aware; considers implementation

Academic Communication: C — Clear structure; minor referencing gaps

Now the student knows exactly where to improve.

The section-based rubric tells me more about what to improve
The capability-based rubric tells me more about what to improve
Both tell me the same amount — just organised differently
