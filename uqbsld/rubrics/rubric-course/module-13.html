Assessment Type Design

Different assessment types carry different pitfalls. Master them: recognise the risks in essays, presentations, group work, and reflection, then design rubrics that mitigate them.

⏱ ~20 minutes
The Challenge

Every Assessment Type Has Blind Spots

Essays invite word-count padding. Presentations reward delivery over substance. Group projects hide free-riding. Reflective tasks can become confessional rather than analytical. Your rubric must anticipate these pitfalls and design them out.

The design principle:

Once you name a pitfall, you can design criteria that sidestep it. This module teaches you to diagnose common rubric mistakes and rewrite criteria that are robust across assessment types.

Activity 4 of 5

Reflection Quality vs Disclosure

Two reflective excerpts from the same assignment. One is deeply personal but analytically shallow. The other shows moderate disclosure but rich analysis. Which deserves the higher grade?

4
Analyse Reflection Quality
Excerpt A: Deep Disclosure, Shallow Analysis
This module was the hardest thing I've done. When we discussed failure, I broke down because I'm terrified of failing. My father always said I wasn't good enough, and that haunts me every day. This assignment made me realise I need therapy. I felt so vulnerable sharing my struggles with the class, but I'm grateful to feel less alone.
Excerpt B: Moderate Disclosure, Rich Analysis
I recognised I often avoid risk-taking in assignments. Reflecting on the framework we studied (Dweck's growth mindset), I see I was operating in a fixed mindset: "I'm either good at this or not." The module's emphasis on iterative improvement helped me reframe failure as data. Going forward, I'll build in multiple drafts rather than trying to be perfect on the first attempt.
