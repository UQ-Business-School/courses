What Makes a Rubric Work?

You already know rubrics are marking grids. But a rubric that truly works serves two very different audiences at the same time — and most don't.

⏱ ~15 minutes
The Scenario

Meet MGTS2501

Throughout this course, you'll work with a single fictional assessment — a case study analysis for MGTS2501 Organisational Behaviour. The rubric you'll see in a moment is typical of what many courses use. By the time you finish Module 20, you'll have transformed it into something genuinely useful.

The assessment: Assessment 2 — Case Study Analysis (30%). Students analyse a case study about a mid-size organisation experiencing high staff turnover. They must identify underlying causes using OB theory, evaluate management's response, and propose evidence-based recommendations. 2,000 words, minimum 8 peer-reviewed sources, APA 7 referencing.

Activity 1 of 3

Two Audiences, One Document

A rubric must work for two very different people: the student trying to improve, and the marker trying to assign a fair grade. Let's see how well these descriptors serve each.

1
The Dual Audience Test

Below are three descriptors from different rubrics. First, read each as a student — how helpful is it for improving your work? Then read each as a marker — how confident would you be in assigning consistent grades?

Descriptor A: "Demonstrates excellent critical analysis of the case study."

As a student (helpful?) 3
As a marker (consistent?) 3

Descriptor B: "Identifies the root causes of the organisational problem, supports each with at least two relevant theories, and explains how they interact."

As a student (helpful?) 3
As a marker (consistent?) 3

Descriptor C: "Good analysis with some relevant theory."

As a student (helpful?) 3
As a marker (consistent?) 3

What you probably noticed:

Descriptor B tends to score highest for both audiences. It tells the student exactly what to do (identify root causes, support with theories, explain interactions) and gives the marker clear, checkable criteria.

Descriptors A and C share the same problem: they rely on vague quality words ("excellent," "good," "some") that mean different things to different people. A student reading "demonstrates excellent critical analysis" still doesn't know what excellent analysis looks like. A marker reading "good analysis with some relevant theory" has to decide what "good" and "some" mean — and their colleague might decide differently.

The takeaway: The best rubric descriptors serve both audiences simultaneously by describing observable actions rather than quality judgements.

What Grade Would You Give?

Using this rubric, what grade would you give?

Four markers graded the same student work using this rubric. What grade do you think the student deserves?

High Distinction (HD) — The work is clearly excellent across all criteria.
Distinction (D) — It's very good overall, just not quite top-tier.
Credit (C) — It's solid but not exceptional work.
Pass (P) — It meets the basic requirements but nothing more.

Here's the problem: All four answers are defensible. The student description we gave you maps plausibly onto Credit, Distinction, or even Pass — because the rubric descriptors don't tell you what any of those quality labels actually mean.

When four markers can't agree on whether the same work is a Pass or a Distinction, the rubric isn't doing its job. This isn't a marker quality problem — it's a rubric quality problem.

In fact, the four "markers" in this scenario gave: HD, D, C, and P — a three-grade spread for identical work. That's exactly what happens when descriptors are just adjectives.

Over the next 19 modules, you'll learn exactly how to fix this. Let's start in Module 2 with the root cause: the Adverb Trap.
