Fail Levels and Formatting

Most rubrics collapse failure into a single "Fail" grade. UQ rubrics use three: Minimal Fail (47–49%), Fail (30–46%), and Low Fail (0–29%). Each has implications for supplementary assessment and what the work actually looks like.

⏱ ~15 minutes
Overview

Three Fail Zones, Not One

Understanding fail-level implications changes how you write failure descriptors. Each zone has different assessment rules and different observable work characteristics.

MF: Minimal Fail
47–49%
Eligible for supplementary assessment. Work is close to passing but falls short on one or two criteria. With targeted revision, could reach Pass standard.
F: Fail
30–46%
Not eligible for supplementary. Work has significant gaps or misunderstandings. Multiple criteria not met. Requires substantial re-engagement.
LF: Low Fail
0–29%
Significant non-submission or fundamental misalignment. Work may be incomplete, off-topic, or show fundamental misunderstanding of the task.

Write Substantive Fail Descriptors

The existing rubric has "Does not demonstrate analysis" at Fail. That's too vague. Rewrite to show what MF, F, and LF work actually looks like for "Critical Analysis."

2
Differentiate Across Three Fail Zones

Use these models as a guide, then write your own versions.

Model: Minimal Fail (47–49%)

Identifies one or two relevant factors but analysis is superficial or partially incorrect. Some relevant theory mentioned but not applied to the case. With additional development, could reach Pass standard.

Model: Fail (30–46%)

Attempts analysis but misidentifies causes or applies theory incorrectly. Response is largely descriptive rather than analytical. Significant gaps between claims and evidence.

Model: Low Fail (0–29%)

No meaningful analysis attempted. Response may restate the question, provide only personal opinion, or be substantially incomplete. Fundamental misunderstanding of the task.

Negative Definition: Credit vs. Distinction

A powerful technique: define the boundary between grades by stating what Credit does not require. "Unlike Distinction, Credit does not...". This removes ambiguity.
