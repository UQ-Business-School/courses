The Adverb Trap

The single most common rubric failure mode — and once you see it, you'll never be able to un-see it.

⏱ ~15 minutes
Activity 1 of 4

Spot the Pattern

Below are five rubric rows from different courses (anonymised). For each row, click every word that changes between grade levels. Look for what the rows have in common — and which one is different.

D: Highly effective team contribution.

Row 5: Evidence Use

HD: Selects sources that challenge the argument, not just support it; synthesises across 3+ perspectives to build an original position.
D: Draws on multiple perspectives and identifies tensions between them; begins to synthesise rather than summarise.
C: Uses relevant sources to support each main point; sources are appropriately cited but treated independently rather than in dialogue.
P: Includes required number of sources; sources support the argument but are descriptive rather than analytical.

0 words highlighted

Rows 1–4 all follow the same pattern: the only words that change are adverbs and adjectives — excellent, very good, good, satisfactory, outstanding, adequate, comprehensive, basic... The underlying sentence stays identical. This is the Adverb Trap.

Row 5 is fundamentally different. Every grade level describes a qualitatively different kind of work — not a different quality rating of the same work. At HD, the student challenges their own argument and synthesises across perspectives. At Pass, they include sources descriptively. These are different actions, not different adjectives.

This is the core distinction you'll learn to apply: differentiate by what the student does, not by how well you think they did it.

Activity 2 of 4

The Adverb Audit

Let's look at the MGTS2501 rubric's "Analysis" row specifically. Click on every adverb or adjective that is doing the work of differentiating grades.

2
Audit the Analysis Row

Click each quality word. How many are there?

HD: Demonstrates excellent critical analysis
D: Demonstrates very good critical analysis
C: Demonstrates good critical analysis
P: Demonstrates satisfactory analysis
F: Does not demonstrate analysis

0 words highlighted

Four adverbs. That's it. This row has five grade levels differentiated by exactly one word each: excellent, very good, good, satisfactory — and then a binary fail ("does not demonstrate").

A marker reading "demonstrates excellent critical analysis" and "demonstrates very good critical analysis" has no objective basis for choosing between them. What is the boundary between "excellent" and "very good"? It's whatever the marker feels it is. And the next marker will feel differently.

This is why rubrics need to describe what the student does at each level — not just attach a quality label.

With the adverb rubric, markers are spread across all four levels — a three-grade spread. With the observable rubric (where descriptors describe what the student actually does at each level), 18 of 20 markers cluster at Credit or Distinction. The rubric did the thinking for them.

This is simulated data, but the pattern is well-documented in assessment research. Observable descriptors produce dramatically more consistent marking.
