Values of Inquiry

Nine intellectual values that give you a precise vocabulary for what you actually mean when you write "thorough" or "clear" or "comprehensive."

⏱ ~20 minutes
The Framework

Why Do We Need This?

In Modules 2–3, you saw that adverbs like "excellent" and "thorough" are meaningless in rubrics. But when we strip them away, what do we replace them with? The Values of Inquiry framework gives us the answer: nine specific intellectual qualities that describe what we're actually looking for in academic work.

Click each value below to learn its definition and how to spot it in student work.

Clarity

Click to expand
Definition: Free from ambiguity. Key terms are defined; claims are unambiguous.
Observable as: Precise terminology; defined key terms; unambiguous claims.

Accuracy

Click to expand
Definition: Factually correct. Facts, figures, and citations are valid.
Observable as: Correct facts and figures; valid theory application; accurate citations.

Precision

Click to expand
Definition: The exact detail required for the context — specific rather than vague.
Observable as: Specific claims; quantified where appropriate; avoids overgeneralisation.

Breadth

Click to expand
Definition: Multiple perspectives considered. Range of viewpoints acknowledged.
Observable as: Multiple source types; alternative viewpoints; cross-disciplinary connections.

Depth

Click to expand
Definition: Underlying complexity explored. Goes beneath the surface.
Observable as: Root causes explored; assumptions examined; nuance and complexity present.

Relevance

Click to expand
Definition: All content pertains to the matter at hand. No tangential material.
Observable as: Every section serves the argument; no padding; stays on topic.

Significance

Click to expand
Definition: Addresses what matters most. Core issues prioritised over trivial ones.
Observable as: Key issues given proportional weight; trivial points not overemphasised.

Coherence

Click to expand
Definition: Parts fit together into a consistent whole. No contradictions.
Observable as: Clear structure; consistent argument throughout; no internal contradictions.

Cogency

Click to expand
Definition: Well-reasoned and persuasive. Evidence supports claims; logic is sound.
Observable as: Claims supported by evidence; logical progression; conclusions follow from premises.

Activity 3 of 4

Build a VoI Descriptor

For the MGTS2501 "Critical Analysis" criterion at Distinction level, write a descriptor that specifically requires Breadth and Coherence but not necessarily Depth.

3
Write with VoI Precision

Your brief: Write the Distinction-level descriptor for "Critical Analysis" in the MGTS2501 case study rubric. The descriptor must require Breadth (multiple perspectives) and Coherence (parts fit together), but should not require Depth (that's what distinguishes HD from D).

Model Answer Identifies multiple contributing factors to the turnover problem, drawing on at least two theoretical perspectives. Analysis connects these factors into a coherent explanation, showing how they relate to each other. Evaluates management's response against the theoretical framework. Does not yet examine underlying assumptions or systemic root causes (which would demonstrate Depth).
How close?

Notice: The model answer explicitly names what the Distinction level does NOT require (Depth). This technique — using the absence of a VoI value to distinguish adjacent grades — is one of the most powerful tools in your rubric design toolkit. You'll use it extensively in later modules.
