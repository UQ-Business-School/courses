# Introduction

A rubric is only as useful as its ability to do two things: help **students** understand what they're aiming for **before** they submit, and help **markers** reach consistent grades **independently**. If a rubric can't do both, it's not doing its job.

These two functions - feedforward for students and reliability for markers - are not separate goals. Both depend on the same underlying principle: **transparency**. Descriptors that describe observable differences in what students actually produce make quality criteria explicit - and that explicitness serves both purposes, even if the pathways are distinct (Panadero et al., 2023).

This guide introduces the frameworks and principles that underpin effective rubric design. It draws on the Values of Inquiry (Value of Inquiry) framework developed by Dr Peter Ellerton at the UQ Critical Thinking Project, the SOLO Taxonomy from Biggs and Collis, and practical design principles refined through extensive rubric development across UQ Business School programs. Value of Inquiry and SOLO are presented here as useful frameworks for generating substantive descriptors - not the only approaches, but ones we have found consistently productive in practice.

The guide is structured around six core topics:

- **The core problem** - why most rubrics default to adverb scaling and why it matters.
- **The Values of Inquiry** - a language for evaluating the quality of student thinking.
- **SOLO Taxonomy** - describing structural complexity and mapping the full UQ 7-point grading scale.
- **Writing observable descriptors** - the spine-and-distinguisher structure and worked examples.

**5-6. Structural design and accessibility** - capability-based criteria and writing for diverse student populations.

**7\. Grade distributions** - what your grade profile tells you about your rubric.

Whether you're writing a rubric from scratch or reviewing one you've used for years, the principles in this guide apply to any assessment type - essays, presentations, group work, or professional artefacts.

# The Core Problem: Adverb Scaling

The single most common issue in rubric design is what we call **adverb scaling** - what the assessment literature refers to as "vague descriptors" (Li & Lindsey, 2015) or "evaluative language" (Sadler, 2009) - differentiating grades by swapping adjectives (_excellent_ → _very good_ → _good_ → _satisfactory_) rather than describing what the student actually **does differently** at each level.

Adverb scaling is so prevalent because it's easy to write. Once you've described what "excellent" looks like, you can quickly generate lower grades by softening the language. But this convenience comes at a real cost: it produces rubrics that are **subjective for markers** (what does "very good" mean to you versus your colleague?) and **opaque for students** (how do I move from "good" to "very good"?).

## What Adverb Scaling Looks Like

Consider a common rubric row for Critical Analysis in a case study report:

|     | **High Distinction (7)** | **Distinction (6)** | **Credit (5)** | **Pass (4)** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Critical Analysis** | Demonstrates **excellent** critical analysis of the case with **outstanding** depth | Demonstrates **very good** critical analysis of the case with **considerable** depth | Demonstrates **good** critical analysis of the case with **adequate** depth | Demonstrates **satisfactory** critical analysis of the case with **basic** depth |


The only things changing across grade levels are the adverbs. A student reading this has no concrete guidance on what to **do** to move from a Pass to a Distinction. A marker has no observable criteria to anchor their judgement - the boundary between "very good" and "good" is entirely subjective.

## What Observable Descriptors Look Like

Now consider the same criterion rewritten with observable descriptors:

|     | **High Distinction (7)** | **Distinction (6)** | **Credit (5)** | **Pass (4)** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Critical Analysis** | Integrates theory to explain **why** issues occur; connects factors to show how they reinforce each other; identifies limitations in the theory applied | Applies theory to explain issues; identifies relationships between factors; analysis is coherent and well-structured | Applies relevant theory to the case; addresses multiple factors but treats them somewhat independently | Links case to theory but connections are surface-level; factors listed rather than analysed |


Each grade now describes a **qualitatively different action**. At Pass, the student lists factors. At Credit, they address multiple factors but independently. At Distinction, they identify relationships between factors. At HD, they explain causal mechanisms and critique the theory itself. A student can read this and know exactly what to do to improve - and two markers can independently reach the same grade.

## The Reliability Test

A useful test for any rubric is to ask two questions. First: **could a student use this rubric to improve their work before submission?** If your descriptors say "excellent" and "satisfactory," the answer is no. Second: **would two markers independently reach the same grade?** If the descriptors rely on subjective terms, markers will interpret them differently, and moderation becomes an argument about adjectives rather than evidence.

The frameworks in the next two sections provide systematic tools for replacing adverb scaling with descriptors that pass both tests.

# The Values of Inquiry

The Values of Inquiry (Value of Inquiry) framework, developed by Dr Peter Ellerton at the UQ Critical Thinking Project, provides **a language for evaluating the quality of thinking**. The framework identifies eight values - things we value in the act of inquiry - that can be used to give precise, actionable feedback on student work and to write rubric descriptors that differentiate grades in meaningful ways. We have found Value of Inquiry consistently productive as a descriptor-writing tool, though it should be noted that it has not been independently validated for this purpose in published research. It is one useful framework among several, including the AAC&U VALUE Rubrics and Sadler's (2009) work on qualitative standards.

The framework builds on the "Intellectual Standards" identified by Elder and Paul (2001), but Ellerton deliberately uses the term _values_ rather than _standards_ for important reasons. Standards suggest benchmarks - binary pass/fail thresholds. Values, by contrast, are general, overlapping, and contextually applied. Just as a graphic equaliser adjusts different frequencies for different types of music, different types of inquiry require different mixes of values. This flexibility is precisely what makes Value of Inquiry so useful for rubric design across different assessment types and disciplines (Ellerton, 2018).

## The Eight Values

Each value represents a quality we look for in student thinking and communication. The table below summarises all eight values with their definitions and the kinds of questions each value prompts.

| **Value** | **Definition** | **Guiding Questions** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Clarity** | Ensuring the audience can understand what we mean; making points as clear as possible. | Is the argument structure clear? Are words well-defined and unambiguous? Are examples useful? |
| **Accuracy** | Representing all information correctly and closely aligned with its original meaning. | Are claims justified? Is what you are saying true? Have you represented ideas faithfully? |
| **Precision** | Being specific and intentional with language and terminology to remove potential for misunderstanding. | Have you used technical terms appropriately? Have you quantified where appropriate? Is vagueness minimised? |
| **Depth** | Providing detailed information and explanations to thoroughly develop points. | Are complexities sufficiently described? Have problematic aspects been identified and dealt with? |
| **Breadth** | Covering a diverse range of directly relevant content and considerations. | Have you considered alternative perspectives? Why have you preferred one perspective over another? |
| **Relevance** | Choosing information that relates directly to the points being developed, without distraction. | Have you focused on the point at issue? Is distracting or unhelpful information minimised? |
| **Significance** | Discussing the most important information related to the topic; focusing on key ideas. | Have you avoided superficial issues? Have you identified and developed your core ideas? |
| **Coherence** | Structuring arguments in a way that makes logical sense, with each point building towards intended meaning. | Are ideas developed logically? Do all premises support your conclusions? Are logical fallacies avoided? |

_Table 1: The eight Values of Inquiry. Adapted from Ellerton (2018), drawing on Elder and Paul (2001)._

## Why Value of Inquiry Works for Rubric Design

The core insight from Value of Inquiry is that these values provide **feedback on the quality of cognitive skill use**. A student may be performing the cognitive skill of analysis, but that doesn't mean they're doing it well. Value of Inquiry gives us the vocabulary to say _how_ it falls short or excels: the analysis might lack **depth** (complexities aren't addressed), or **breadth** (alternative perspectives are ignored), or **coherence** (the logical structure doesn't hold together).

This translates directly to rubric descriptors. Instead of scaling adverbs, we describe which Value of Inquiry values are present and to what degree. Consider how Value of Inquiry values differentiate an "Evidence Use" criterion across grade levels:

### Worked Example: Evidence Use in a Research Essay
| Grade | Descriptor |
| ---: | :------------------------- |
| **HD:** | Sources are selected for their **significance** to the argument; evidence is integrated with **precision** (paraphrased accurately, cited correctly); counter-evidence is addressed with **breadth** and **depth** |
| **D:** | Sources are **relevant** and **accurately** represented; evidence supports claims with **clarity**; some counter-evidence acknowledged |
| **C:** | Sources are **relevant** but used descriptively rather than analytically; evidence-claim connections lack **precision** |
| **P:** | Sources are present but selection lacks **relevance** or **significance**; evidence is listed rather than integrated; **accuracy** of representation is inconsistent |

Notice how each grade level is differentiated by which values are present, absent, or developing - not by adverbs. The Value of Inquiry vocabulary does the work that "excellent/good/satisfactory" was trying to do, but with specificity that both students and markers can act on.

## The Overlap Principle

An important feature of Value of Inquiry is that the values **overlap and interact**. Precision can contribute to clarity. Depth of analysis benefits from coherence. Breadth without relevance produces unfocused work. This means that when writing descriptors, you don't need to address every value in every cell

- you select the values most salient to the criterion and the assessment type. Ellerton uses the analogy of a graphic equaliser: just as different genres of music benefit from different frequency profiles, different types of inquiry foreground different values (Ellerton, 2018).

For a quantitative analysis task, you might foreground **precision** and **accuracy**. For a stakeholder analysis, **breadth** and **significance** might matter most. For a literature review, **depth**, **coherence**, and **relevance** are typically central. The framework adapts to the assessment rather than imposing a rigid template.

# Structure of the Observed Learning Outcome (SOLO) Taxonomy

The **Structure of the Observed Learning Outcome (SOLO) Taxonomy**, developed by Biggs and Collis (1982), describes five levels of increasing structural complexity in student responses. Where Value of Inquiry provides a language for the _quality_ of thinking, SOLO describes the _structure_ - how students organise and connect ideas.

SOLO is particularly useful for rubric design because it describes a natural progression in how students handle complexity, and this progression maps well to the UQ grading scale.

## The UQ 7-Point Grading Scale

Before mapping SOLO to grade levels, it helps to have the full UQ grading scale in view. Each grade corresponds to a percentage range and carries specific implications - most critically, the distinction between grades 3 and 2 determines supplementary assessment eligibility.

| **Grade** | **Descriptor** | **Percentage** | **Implications for Rubric Design** |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :--- |
| **7** | High Distinction | 85-100% | Descriptors should reflect work that goes beyond the expected: critiques, synthesises, or extends. At least some students should be able to reach this level. |
| **6** | Distinction | 75-84% | Strong integration and connection. Work demonstrates relational or extended abstract thinking with consistent quality. |
| **5** | Credit | 65-74% | Competent work that addresses the task with reasonable breadth but may lack the integration or depth of higher grades. |
| **4** | Pass | 50-64% | Meets minimum requirements. Descriptors should clearly define the threshold: what does "good enough" look like? |
| **3** | **Fail** | 45-49% | **Supplementary threshold.** Students receiving a 3 are eligible for supplementary assessment. Rubrics must distinguish between work that narrowly misses the Pass threshold and work that falls well short of it. |
| **2** | **Fail** | 20-44% | Significant gaps in understanding or task completion. The response attempts the task but falls substantially short of requirements. |
| **1** | **Fail** | 0-19% | No meaningful engagement with the task, or work that is entirely off-topic, plagiarised, or not submitted. |

_Table 2: The UQ 7-point grading scale with percentage ranges and rubric design implications._

**Why Fail Grades Matter in Rubric Design**

Under UQ's _Assessment Procedures_, students who receive a grade of **3** (45-49%) are eligible for supplementary assessment - a second attempt to demonstrate they can pass. Students who receive a 2 or 1 are **not** eligible. This means rubrics must differentiate between "narrowly missed the threshold" (grade 3) and "substantially below expectations" (grades 1-2). If your rubric only has a single "Fail" column, markers cannot make this distinction - and students may be denied their supplementary assessment rights.

## The Five SOLO Levels

| **SOLO Level** | **Description** | **Indicative UQ Grade** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-structural** | The student misses the point or uses irrelevant information. The response does not address the task. | Fail: 1-3 (0-49%) |
| **Uni-structural** | The student focuses on one relevant aspect. The response addresses the task but from a single perspective. | Pass: 4 (50-64%) |
| **Multi-structural** | The student identifies several relevant aspects but treats them independently. The response lists or catalogues without connecting. | Pass-Credit: 4-5 (50-74%) |
| **Relational** | The student integrates aspects into a coherent whole. Relationships, causes, and effects are identified. The response demonstrates understanding of the structure. | Credit-Distinction: 5-6 (65-84%) |
| **Extended Abstract** | The student goes beyond the given information to generalise, hypothesise, or critique. The response connects to broader principles, identifies limitations, or proposes novel applications. | Distinction-HD: 6-7 (75-100%) |

_Table 3: SOLO Taxonomy levels mapped to the UQ grading scale. Based on Biggs and Collis (1982)._

## SOLO in Rubric Descriptors

SOLO provides the **architecture** for grade-level differentiation. The key progression is from **listing** (multi-structural: "identifies several factors") through **connecting** (relational: "explains how factors interact") to **critiquing** (extended abstract: "identifies limitations in the theory applied"). This is a qualitative shift in cognitive operation, not just "more" of the same thing.

Consider how SOLO structures a Problem Identification criterion for a case study:

### Worked Example: Problem Identification (SOLO Progression)

| Grade | Descriptor |
| ---: | :------------------------- |
| **HD (Extended Abstract):** | Identifies issues not explicitly stated in the case; connects them to broader organisational and contextual factors; distinguishes symptoms from root causes and prioritises by impact |
| **D (Relational):** | Identifies key issues accurately and connects them to the broader organisational context; explains how issues relate to one another |
| **C (Multi-structural):** | Identifies key issues within the relevant context; addresses multiple issues but treats them somewhat independently |
| **P (Uni-structural):** | Identifies issues stated in the case; addresses the task from a single perspective without contextualisation |
| **F-3 (Pre-structural):** | Attempts to identify issues but misinterprets the case or focuses on peripheral details; the core problem is not addressed |
| **F-1-2 (Pre-structural):** | Does not identify relevant issues; the response does not engage with the case or is entirely off-topic

The SOLO progression is visible: at _Pass_, the student works with what's explicitly given (uni-structural). At Credit, they catalogue multiple issues (multi-structural). At Distinction, they connect them (relational). At HD, they go beyond the given material to identify implicit issues and prioritise (extended abstract).

## How Value of Inquiry and SOLO Work Together

Value of Inquiry and SOLO are complementary lenses, not competing ones. In practice:

**SOLO establishes the grade-level architecture** - it tells you _what kind of cognitive operation_ to describe at each level (listing, connecting, critiquing).

**Value of Inquiry provides the descriptor language** - it tells you _what qualities to look for_ within that operation (depth, breadth, precision, coherence).

A descriptor that uses both might read: "Integrates theory to explain _why_ issues occur \[relational/extended abstract SOLO level\]; connects factors to show how they reinforce each other \[**coherence**\]; identifies limitations in the theory applied \[**depth**, **breadth**\]."

You don't need to consciously label every element with its framework origin - the goal is to internalise both lenses so that your descriptors naturally describe observable, qualitatively different actions at each grade level.

# Writing Observable Descriptors

With Value of Inquiry and SOLO as your frameworks, the practical task is translating them into rubric cells. This section introduces what we call the **spine-and-distinguisher** structure - our working term for what the rubric design literature calls "parallel construction" (Brookhart, 2013) or "coherence across descriptor levels" (CLASS, 1997). It is a technique for writing descriptor sets that are consistent, readable, and genuinely differentiating.

## The Spine-and-Distinguisher Structure

Strong descriptor sets share a **spine** - a common sentence structure or opening that stays consistent across grade levels - with **distinguishers** that change to indicate the qualitative shift. The spine anchors the reader, making it immediately clear what aspect is being assessed. The distinguishers show what changes across grades.

**Example: Spine (underlined) with Distinguishers**

**HD:** Identifies issues at multiple levels; distinguishes symptoms from root causes; prioritises by impact

**D:** Identifies issues accurately and connects them to the broader organisational context

**C:** Identifies issues stated in the case within the relevant context

**P:** Identifies issues stated in the case, though without contextualisation

The spine ("Identifies issues") anchors the reader. The distinguishers show what changes: how far beyond the case the student looks (**SOLO level**), the quality of engagement with complexity (**Value of Inquiry: depth**), and whether they prioritise what matters most (**Value of Inquiry: significance**).

## A Process for Writing Descriptors

When writing or revising descriptors, the following process works consistently:

**Step 1: Name the capability being assessed.** What cognitive skill is this criterion asking the student to demonstrate? Analysis? Application? Evaluation? Communication?

**Step 2: Identify what "excellent" looks like in observable terms.** Describe the HD response. What would you actually see in the student's work? Use Value of Inquiry to guide you - which values are fully present at this level?

**Step 3: Identify what "minimally acceptable" looks like.** Describe the Pass response. What is the threshold? Which Value of Inquiry values are present but limited? What SOLO level is the student operating at?

**Step 4: Fill in the middle grades.** Use SOLO progression to structure the journey from Pass to HD. What changes at each step? Map the qualitative shifts - listing → connecting → integrating → critiquing.

**Step 5: Check the spine.** Read across the row. Is there a consistent structure that makes the progression visible? If you underline the common elements, do the distinguishers tell a coherent story?

**Step 6: Check for adverb creep.** Review each descriptor. If the only difference between two adjacent grades is an adjective or adverb ("thoroughly" vs "adequately"), rewrite to describe an observable difference in what the student produces.

## Worked Examples Across Assessment Types

The following examples demonstrate how Value of Inquiry and SOLO produce observable descriptors across different types of assessment. Each example shows the same criterion rewritten from adverb scaling to observable descriptors.

### Example 1: Depth of Knowledge in a Research Essay

|     | **HD (7)** | **D (6)** | **C (5)** | **P (4)** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Before** | _Demonstrates excellent depth of knowledge_ | _Demonstrates very good depth of knowledge_ | _Demonstrates good depth of knowledge_ | _Demonstrates satisfactory depth of knowledge_ |
| **After** | Engages with current debates and contested positions in the literature; identifies where scholars disagree and evaluates the strength of competing arguments | Draws on a range of perspectives including recent scholarship; explains how different theoretical positions relate to one another | Accurately represents key concepts and theoretical positions from the literature; coverage is adequate but relies on foundational texts | Identifies relevant theories but understanding is surface-level; may contain inaccuracies or rely heavily on a narrow range of sources |

_Value of Inquiry values in play: depth (engaging with complexity), breadth (range of perspectives), accuracy (faithful representation), significance (contested positions rather than settled knowledge)._

### Example 2: Communication Effectiveness in a Group Presentation

|     | **HD (7)** | **D (6)** | **C (5)** | **P (4)** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Before** | _Outstanding presentation skills with excellent engagement_ | _Very good presentation skills with strong engagement_ | _Good presentation skills with adequate engagement_ | _Satisfactory presentation skills with basic engagement_ |
| **After** | Key messages are immediately apparent; visual aids reinforce rather than duplicate spoken content; delivery is adapted to audience reactions; transitions between speakers are seamless | Structure guides the audience through a logical progression; visual aids support the argument; language is appropriate to the audience; speakers build on each other's points | Content is clearly structured; visual aids are present and relevant; language is generally appropriate though occasionally too technical or informal | Content is presented in a recognisable structure; visual aids are present but may be text-heavy or disconnected from the spoken content; delivery is read from notes |

_Value of Inquiry values in play: clarity (audience can follow the argument), coherence (logical progression), relevance (visual aids serve the argument), precision (language appropriate to audience)._

### Example 3: Application to Professional Practice

|     | **HD (7)** | **D (6)** | **C (5)** | **P (4)** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Before** | _Excellent application of theory to professional practice_ | _Very good application of theory to professional practice_ | _Good application of theory to practice_ | _Satisfactory application of theory to practice_ |
| **After** | Recommendations follow logically from the analysis; each is justified with evidence rather than opinion; implementation considers constraints, risks, and stakeholder impacts | Recommendations are grounded in the analysis and supported by evidence; practical considerations are addressed; connections between theory and proposed actions are explicit | Recommendations are relevant to the case and linked to theoretical concepts; practical detail is present but may be generic rather than tailored to the specific context | Recommendations are stated but the connection to theory or analysis is unclear; practical detail is limited; recommendations could apply to any similar case without modification |

_Value of Inquiry values in play: coherence (recommendations follow from analysis), accuracy (evidence rather than opinion), depth (implementation considers constraints), significance (tailored to context rather than generic)._

# Structural Design: Capabilities, Not Sections

Beyond descriptor quality, the **structure** of the rubric matters. A common issue is organising rubric rows by **sections of the assessment** (Introduction, Literature Review, Analysis, Conclusion) rather than by **capabilities** the student is demonstrating (Critical Analysis, Application to Practice, Academic Communication).

## Why Section-Based Rubrics Are Problematic

Section-based criteria create several issues. The same capability often gets assessed in multiple rows - critical analysis might appear in "Literature Review," "Analysis," and "Discussion," each with slightly different standards. This makes it difficult for markers to maintain consistency and inflates the rubric without improving its diagnostic power.

Section-based criteria also anchor students to a rigid structure rather than rewarding the quality of their thinking. A student who integrates their literature and analysis into a more sophisticated argument structure may actually be penalised if the rubric expects separate sections.

## Capability-Based Criteria

Capability-based criteria ask: **what can the student do?** Each row assesses a distinct skill, weighted by importance to the learning outcomes. This approach has several advantages:

**Clarity:** Each criterion assesses one thing, making it easier for both markers and students to understand what's being evaluated.

**Flexibility:** Students can organise their work in whatever way best serves their argument, rather than being locked into a prescribed structure.

**Alignment:** Capability-based criteria map naturally to program-level learning outcomes and graduate attributes, supporting institutional reporting.

**Versatility:** The same set of capability-based criteria can work across different assessment formats within a course, with descriptors adjusted for context.

## A Structural Comparison

The following shows the same assessment - a case study report - structured both ways:

| **Section-Based (Before)** | **Capability-Based (After)** |
| --- | --- |
| **Row 1:** Introduction (10%)<br>**Row 2:** Literature Review (20%)<br>**Row 3:** Analysis (30%)<br>**Row 4:** Recommendations (20%)<br>**Row 5:** Conclusion (10%)<br>**Row 6:** Referencing (10%) | **Row 1:** Problem Identification (20%)<br>**Row 2:** Critical Analysis (35%)<br>**Row 3:** Application to Practice (25%)<br>**Row 4:** Academic Communication (20%) |
| _6 rows mirroring document structure. Critical analysis assessed in Rows 2, 3, and arguably 4. Introduction and Conclusion rows are hard to differentiate across grades._ | _4 rows assessing distinct capabilities. Each row has a clear, differentiable progression. Weightings reflect importance to learning outcomes._ |

The capability-based version is not only more efficient (four rows instead of six) but each row is genuinely assessable with observable descriptors. It's difficult to write meaningfully different HD and Pass descriptors for an "Introduction" criterion, but straightforward for "Problem Identification."

# Writing for Diverse Student Populations

UQ's student body includes a significant proportion of students from culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) backgrounds. Rubric language that is idiomatic, culturally specific, or unnecessarily complex creates barriers that have nothing to do with the student's capability in the assessed skill.

## Practical Guidelines

**Use plain, direct language.** Replace "cogently proposes innovative solutions" with "solutions follow logically from the analysis; each recommendation is justified with evidence." The second version is longer but vastly easier to parse for a non-native English speaker.

**Avoid idiomatic expressions.** Phrases like "hits the mark," "scrapes by," or "leaves much to be desired" are culturally loaded and imprecise. Describe what the student produces, not how it makes you feel.

**Define technical terms at first use.** If your rubric uses discipline-specific terminology (e.g., "stakeholder analysis," "sensitivity testing"), ensure the assessment description or a supporting resource explains what these mean.

**Be specific about quantity where it matters.** If you expect a minimum number of peer-reviewed sources, state it. "Extensive use of the literature" is subjective; "Draws on at least 12 peer-reviewed sources" is concrete. Use count anchors at the Pass threshold and quality descriptors above it.

**Ensure each cell can stand alone.** A student should be able to read a single cell (e.g., Distinction for Critical Analysis) and understand what's expected without needing to cross-reference other cells. Avoid descriptors that rely on comparison ("better than Credit-level work").

# A Note on Grade Distributions

Rubric design and grade distributions are connected. If an assessment consistently produces grades clustered at one end of the scale, the rubric and assessment task should be reviewed - the issue is almost certainly with design, not students.

## UQ Expectations

UQ does not impose a forced distribution, but provides strong guidance on expected grade profiles. These expectations apply at the course level across a cohort:

| **Guideline** | **What This Means** |
| --- | --- |
| **No more than 35%** of students should receive a 6 or 7 | If most students are getting Distinctions and HDs, the assessment may not be demanding enough, or the rubric may not differentiate upper grades effectively. |
| **The majority** of students should receive a 4 or 5 | This is where a well-calibrated assessment centres its cohort. The rubric's Pass and Credit descriptors carry the most weight. |
| **No more than 20%** should fail a course | Excessive failure rates suggest misalignment between teaching, task difficulty, and assessment expectations. Review the rubric's Pass threshold. |
| **At least some students** should be able to achieve a 7 | If no one reaches HD, the rubric's top descriptors may describe an unreachable standard, or the task may not give students scope to demonstrate extended abstract thinking. |

_Table 4: UQ grade distribution guidelines. While not a forced distribution, courses with profiles outside these ranges will be flagged for review._

## What Distributions Tell You About Your Rubric

Each semester, UQ and program teams flag courses where the grade profile deviates significantly from these expectations. If your course is flagged, the rubric is a productive place to start the conversation:

- **Too many high grades (>35% at 6-7):** Check whether your descriptors genuinely differentiate between Credit, Distinction, and HD. If the HD descriptor is essentially "Distinction but more so," the rubric may be collapsing those grades together. Ask whether your task gives enough scope for qualitatively different levels of performance.

- **Too many failures (>20%):** Review the Pass threshold. Is it clearly described? Could a student read the Pass descriptor and know what "good enough" looks like? Also check whether the assessment task aligns with what was actually taught - a rubric can only assess what students had the opportunity to learn.

- **Grades clustered at one level:** If most students receive the same grade, the rubric may lack sufficient differentiation. Use Value of Inquiry values and SOLO levels to create observable qualitative shifts between adjacen grades. The spine-and-distinguisher technique is particularly useful here.

- **No students reaching HD:** Check your HD descriptors. Are they describing genuinely outstanding work, or an impossible standard? HD should represent the top of what is achievable within the task constraints, not a theoretical ideal that no student could reach.

# Quick Self-Check

Use these nine questions to audit your rubric. If you answer "no" to three or more, the rubric would benefit from revision.

| # | Descriptor |
| ---: | :--- |
| **1.** | **Could a student use this rubric to improve their work before submission?** <br>If your descriptors say "excellent" and "satisfactory," the student has no concrete guidance on what to change. |
| **2.** | **Would two markers independently reach the same grade?**<br>If descriptors rely on subjective terms, moderation becomes an argument about adjectives rather than evidence. |
| **3.** | **Does each grade level describe a qualitatively different action?**<br>Moving from "good" to "very good" is not a qualitative shift. Moving from "lists factors" to "explains how factors interact" is. |
| **4.** | **Are your rubric rows organised by capability rather than by section?**<br>If rows mirror assessment headings (Introduction, Body, Conclusion), consider restructuring around what students demonstrate. |
| **5.** | **Can you read across a row and see a clear SOLO progression?**<br>Use the spine-and-distinguisher test: if you underline the common structure, the parts that change should tell a coherent story. |
| **6.** | **Could you identify which Value of Inquiry values each descriptor addresses?**<br>If you can't name at least one value (depth, breadth, clarity, etc.) that a descriptor is assessing, it may be too vague. |
| **7.** | **Would a CALD student understand every descriptor without cultural assumptions?**<br>Check for idioms, ambiguous phrasing, and descriptors that require comparison rather than standing alone. |
| **8.** | **Does your rubric include differentiated fail descriptors?**<br>A single "Fail" column cannot distinguish between work that narrowly misses the Pass threshold<br>(grade 3, supplementary eligible) and work that falls well short (grades 1-2). |
| **9.** | **Is your grade distribution within UQ expectations?**<br>No more than 35% at 6-7; majority at 4-5; no more than 20% failing. If your profile is outside these ranges, review the rubric and task design. |
