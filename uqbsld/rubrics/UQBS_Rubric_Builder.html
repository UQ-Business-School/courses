Rubric Design Fundamentals · Tool

The ATLAS Rubric Builder

Draft and revise marking rubrics that describe what the work actually does differently at each grade, with the Graduate Attribute rules built in so they cannot be broken by accident.

The builder drafts, you shape and check, the coordinator decides. Nothing reaches a marker or a student until two people have judged it.

The rubric builder is one of the tools inside ATLAS, the School's learning design platform. Point it at a course and an assessment, hand it whatever you already have — the existing rubric, the task sheet, your marking notes — and it drafts a rubric where each cell says what a student demonstrably does at that level, rather than swapping "excellent" for "good" down the row. When a coordinator already has a rubric, it remixes rather than scraps: it keeps what works and makes every change traceable to a design principle, so the coordinator still recognises their own rubric in the result.

Who it is for

Learning designers drive the tool. Course coordinators review and approve what it produces. That order is deliberate: the tool drafts, the learning designer shapes and checks, the coordinator owns the assessment and makes the final call. The builder does not approve anything and it does not mark student work.

The three jobs it can do

When you set up a build, the tool asks what the rubric needs to do. There are three answers, and two of them are set by School policy rather than preference. You never choose how a Graduate Attribute sits in the rubric — that follows from the job.

Plain rubric

General build or revision

Capability criteria, observable descriptors, the UQ seven-point scale. No Graduate Attribute involved.

GA woven in

Curriculum uplift

For a mapped major. At least two of a GA's three traits are worked into existing criteria, each asterisked, together worth at least 10% of the marks.

Accreditation

AoL designated assessment

The accreditation case. All three traits, each as its own asterisked row, never merged, together worth at least 10%. This is where the rules are hard.

The accreditation rules are guarantees, not requests

A validate-and-repair pipeline runs in code, not in the prompt. Before any descriptor is written it checks the plan against the AoL rules — three GA rows, one per trait, none merged or folded in, the GA rows at or above 10%, weightings totalling 100 — and sends anything that fails back with the exact violations named. The model reads the task and writes calibrated descriptors; it is never trusted to remember the constraints.

What you get out

A full rubric with every cell editable in place, a short design rationale written for the coordinator (what was preserved and why things changed), and a Word document that follows the School's convention: the course header, the purple grade-band row, asterisks on every GA criterion, and a footer note explaining which GA the asterisk marks. Where the tool does not know a part, such as the assessment number for a hand-entered task, it leaves that part out rather than guessing.

The mapping is a suggestion, not ground truth

The tool reads a point-in-time snapshot of the School's GA mapping and AoL nomination sheets. It is not consistently updated, so nothing applies until you click it, and where a course sits in more than one major its AoL home can be ambiguous. Confirm against the current mapping sheets before relying on it, and tell the Learning Design team if the snapshot disagrees with what you know to be current.
