Disclosing AI use in teaching and learning materials

For anyone who creates course content in the Business School.

This page sets out how we disclose the use of artificial intelligence (AI) in the teaching and learning materials we develop. It is written for course coordinators, but applies to anyone who creates course content: lecture material, slides, readings, activities, quizzes, feedback rubrics and the like.

The short version is that AI is now part of how many of us prepare materials, and we want that to be open rather than hidden. Disclosing AI use is normal practice, not an admission of fault. It tells students and colleagues what they are looking at, and it keeps the author clearly responsible for the result.

One rule that does not move

AI is never used to mark student work. Not to grade it, not to rank it, not to generate the marks or the feedback that determines a grade. This holds regardless of the tool, the platform, or how "closed" the system is. If you are marking, you are marking.

Everything else on this page is about materials you develop, not about assessing students.

The disclosure statement

Where AI has contributed to a piece of material, include the following statement. Use the copy button to grab it as plain text.

Disclosure statement

Artificial intelligence (AI) was used in the development of this material. AI use was limited to a closed, secure system, with consideration for privacy and copyright requirements. All outputs have been subject to editorial review and the author(s) remain responsible for the accuracy, integrity and originality of the work.

Use it as written. It has been reviewed for copyright and privacy, so please do not paraphrase it. If the wording needs to change for your context, check with the team first.

Place it where a reader will see it in context: a footer or acknowledgements slide on a deck, the end matter of a document, or a course-level statement in Blackboard where AI use runs across the whole course. One clear statement covering the material is enough. You do not need to annotate every paragraph.

The statement assumes two things are true. First, that any AI use stayed inside a closed, secure system, meaning a UQ-approved tool rather than a public chatbot you have pasted student data or unpublished content into. Second, that you have read the output, checked it, and stand behind it. If either is not true, fix that before you publish, not the statement.

When to disclose, and when you do not need to

Disclose when AI has shaped the substance of the material: drafting or rewriting explanations, generating examples or scenarios, producing quiz items, summarising sources you then built on, or creating images and diagrams. If AI changed what the material says or contains, say so.

You generally do not need to disclose routine, assistive uses that do not affect substance, such as spelling and grammar checks, predictive text, transcription of your own recording, or search that only pointed you to sources you then read and cited yourself. The line is whether AI contributed content or judgement, or just helped you type.

When you are unsure, disclose. It costs nothing, and it is the behaviour we want to normalise. The point of moving to open disclosure is that no one has to guess what happened behind a piece of material, and no one feels they have to keep quiet about a tool they used in good faith.

A note on responsibility: disclosure does not transfer it. The author still owns the accuracy of every claim, the integrity of the sources, and the originality of the work. AI is a tool you used. You are the person accountable for what it produced.
